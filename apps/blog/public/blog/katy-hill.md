Katy Hill is one of those tunes that turns up everywhere. Old-time jams, bluegrass sets, fiddle contests. If there is a fiddle in the room long enough, someone will eventually call it.

## Where It Comes From

The tune is old, though exactly how old is hard to say. The earliest known recordings date to Allen Sisson in 1925 and Bill Chitwood in 1927. North Georgia fiddler Lowe Stokes cut a version in 1928 that he learned from an Alabama fiddler named Joe Lee, and Stokes's playing had a wide influence on the generation around him.

The tune's reach grew when Fiddlin' Arthur Smith brought it to the Grand Ole Opry. Bill Monroe recorded it in 1940 with Howdy Forrester on fiddle, and that version introduced it to a whole new audience.

## Katy Hill and Sally Johnson

If you have spent any time in old-time circles, you have probably noticed how close Katy Hill sounds to Sally Johnson. Tommy Jarrell put it plainly: he thought both tunes descended from an even older melody called Piney Woods Gal. In older versions the two tunes were distinct enough that players kept them separate. The Grand Ole Opry generation smoothed and simplified until the high strains became nearly identical.

Which came first is still debated. What is clear is that they share a family and that knowing one helps you hear the other differently.
